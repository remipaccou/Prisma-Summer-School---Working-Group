\begin{abstract}
Of the 1,564 pathways in the Scenario Compass Initiative, 497 reach net zero CO$_2$ by 2070, yielding a naive $P(\text{NZ2070}) \approx 32\%$. We evaluate this ensemble against observed 2010--2025 data for six variables. Net-zero scenarios carry significantly larger hindcast errors than non-net-zero ones --- they assumed faster decarbonisation than has occurred. This bias is robust to ensemble rebalancing across model families, to model economic structure, and to project vintage. The naive 32\% likely overstates the probability of reaching net zero by 2070.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{hindcast, scenario ensemble, net zero, integrated assessment models}

% ============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================================

The SCI 2025 ensemble assembles 1,564 IAM pathways from 65 models across 41 research projects spanning 2015--2024. A naive count gives $P(\text{NZ2070}) = 32\%$, but this treats all scenarios as equally credible. We test whether scenarios that reach net zero are systematically less accurate about the recent past, which would indicate the naive estimate is biased upward.

% ============================================================================
\section{Data and Methods}
% ============================================================================

We compare SCI projections at $t \in \{2010, 2015, 2020, 2025\}$ against observed values from the Global Carbon Budget 2025, Energy Institute Statistical Review 2025, IRENA 2026, and IAEA PRIS for six variables: CO$_2$ emissions, coal primary energy, solar PV and wind capacity, nuclear capacity, and GDP (PPP). For each scenario $j$, variable $v$, and year $t$, we compute $\varepsilon_{j,v,t} = y_{v,t}^{\text{obs}} - \hat{y}_{j,v,t}$ and derive ME (bias), MAE (typical error), and RMSE. To correct for ensemble imbalance (five model families contribute 66\% of scenarios), we weight each scenario by $1/n_f$ where $n_f$ is the count from its family (17 families). We further test robustness along two dimensions: model economic structure (endogenous-economy vs.\ energy-system models) and project vintage (early $\leq$2017, mid 2018--2020, late 2021--2024).

\paragraph{A.1 --- NZ scenarios are more wrong.}
NZ2070 scenarios carry ME$_j = +607$ (they systematically underestimate emissions), while non-NZ scenarios are centred at $-23$. The gap is significant (KS $p < 10^{-16}$) and holds for all six variables. Economy models (REMIND, WITCH, AIM, GEM-E3) show a persistent optimistic bias ($\text{ME} = +613$); energy-system models (MESSAGE, IMAGE, POLES) are centred ($-41$) but miss COVID more severely.

\paragraph{A.1 --- Vintage does not explain the bias.}
Recent projects ($\geq$2021) calibrate better at their base year (MAE$_{2010}$: 685 vs 1,141 for pre-2017 projects) but undershoot 2025 \emph{more} (ME$_{2025}$: $+3{,}280$ vs $+466$) because they incorporated more ambitious decarbonisation assumptions. The NZ vs.\ non-NZ gap persists within every vintage group (ME$_j \approx +800$ for NZ vs.\ $\approx 0$ for non-NZ).

\section{Discussion}
% ============================================================================

The hindcast reveals a structural pattern: the ensemble overestimates the pace of near-term decarbonisation, and this bias is concentrated in net-zero scenarios. The finding is robust to model family weighting, economic structure, and project vintage. If the scenarios most likely to be wrong about the recent past are the ones reaching net zero, the naive $P(\text{NZ2070}) \approx 32\%$ is biased upward.

Two caveats apply. First, 2020--2025 includes the COVID shock and its rebound, an exogenous event no scenario was designed to capture. Second, hindcast accuracy over 15~years does not guarantee forecast accuracy over 45~years. Nevertheless, systematic optimism about coal phase-out and emission reductions --- visible across model types and vintages --- points to a structural issue, not a transient one.
