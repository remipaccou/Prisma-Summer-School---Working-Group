## Slide 1 — The question, and our answer up front

**Title:** Can the world reach net-zero CO₂ by 2070 — and is the "32% chance" real?

- 1,564 model simulations; 497 reach net-zero by 2070 → people quote **"32% chance."**
- We confront all of them with what actually happened, 2010–2025.
- **Our answer:** the 32% is not a real probability; the simulations forecast too optimistically; but
  clean technology is getting cheap faster than they assumed — so the blocker isn't cost, it's that
  we add clean energy without removing the dirty.

*Figure:* `working files/co2_overview.png` (all simulations + the real points).
*Say:* "We didn't compute a better probability. We found the question was the wrong one — and a more
useful answer underneath it."

## Slide 2 — The 32% is not a probability

**Title:** A number that moves on its own is not a probability

- It's just **a count of what researchers chose to simulate** — the top 3 labs make 28% of all runs.
- Change the **deadline** and it moves on its own:

  | by 2060 | by 2070 | by 2080 | ever |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | 16% | 31% | 43% | 57% |

- Change **how you count** the simulations: 24% → 31% → 39%.

*Say:* "Two choices that have nothing to do with the climate each move the number by half. So we stop
trying to fix the 32% and instead ask: are these simulations even good forecasts?"

---

## Slide 3 — The simulations forecast badly

**Title:** Tested against reality, the ensemble points too optimistic

- **It expected too little CO₂ in 2025** — it assumed a peak-and-fall that never came. (Not COVID:
  that only explains 2020; 2025 is back on the old rising trend.)
- **The net-zero simulations are the most off** — they assumed an early turn that hasn't started.
- **A dumb "straight-line" rule beats the whole ensemble** on 4 of 6 variables.
- **Reality always lands on the high-emissions side** of the cloud.

*Figure:* `working files/co2_finding1_simple.png` (models bend down, reality keeps rising).
*Say:* "As a forecasting tool it isn't reliable — and it's biased in one direction: too low."

---

## Slide 4 — What we CAN forecast: cost — and the real mechanism

**Title:** Clean tech is winning; the world just isn't switching

- **Cost is the one thing we forecast well.** Solar: ~2,440 → ~250 $/kW (2010→2024), heading to
  ~30-40 by 2070. Cheap whatever happens.
- **The world adds without subtracting:** solar in 2025 is **double** the median model — yet coal hit
  a record.
- So the lock on net-zero is **not the cost of clean tech** (that's beating the models) — it's
  **substitution**: we don't retire the fossils.

*Figure:* `figures/pv_wright_cost_projection_to_2070.png` (cost collapse; net-zero ≈ non-net-zero).
*Say:* "The net-zero simulations aren't too optimistic about technology — if anything too pessimistic.
They're wrong about a fossil phase-out that isn't happening."

---

## Slide 5 — Conclusion: the constraint moves

**Title:** The door to 2070 is opened by technology and held shut by fossil inertia

- Net-zero by 2070 is **not foreclosed** by the data — and **not blocked by clean-tech cost**.
- The honest "corrected share" is **~20%** under any sensible filter — and **can't go lower**: a path
  that decarbonises late is invisible to a 15-year check.
- This is a **hindcast, not a true forecast** (all simulations are post-2017); the real test needs
  older (2014) scenarios.
- **The decisive variable is substitution — policy and system inertia — not cost.**

*Figure (optional):* `working files/partC_sensitivity.png` (~20%, and why no single number).
*Say:* "We don't say net-zero is impossible. We say where the real problem is: stopping fossils, not
making clean energy cheap."

### Backup slides (if pushed)

- **Calibration:** reality at the 90th percentile for solar, 1st for GDP — biased low, overconfident
  where tech moved fastest. (`working files/calibration_pit.png`)
- **Variable selection:** only coal, CO₂, solar discriminate — and they disagree (the addition
  signature). (`figures/partB1_boxplots.png`)
- **Method honesty:** we used Lafond's framework (conditional forecasts, calibration, skill, Wright's
  law); we did not run the heavy machinery — the data is too short (15 years, few points).
- **Two traps we avoid:** "net-zero models are bad" (no — they assumed policy that didn't come) and
  "the share is 20–48%" (no — ~20%; only a poor solar-only filter gives 48%).
